# Stakeholder follow-up

Draft a follow-up message that gets read, restates the deal context, and makes the next step easy to say yes to. Most follow-ups are bad because they read like meeting minutes. Yours won't.

## What to ask before drafting

1. **Who's the recipient?** Single person or thread? Their role and seniority shape the tone.
2. **What was the call?** Discovery, demo, business case review, mutual close plan, exec align. Each follow-up has a different shape.
3. **What's the next step you want them to take?** This is the most important question. There must be exactly one — not three. If the user can't pick one, ask them to commit before you draft.
4. **What did they say that mattered?** Specific quotes or points. The follow-up should reference *their* words, not yours, so it doesn't feel like a template.
5. **Who else is in the deal that's not on the email?** This affects whether to suggest a follow-up call, a doc to share, or an intro request.

## Output format

```
Subject: [{Their company}] {one specific thing tied to the call}

Hi {name},

Thanks for the time today. The thing that stood out: {one specific thing they said, in their words}.

Quick recap of what we covered:
- {point 1}
- {point 2}
- {point 3}

What I'm taking away:
- {1–2 things — show you were listening and made a connection they didn't}

Next step: {ONE clear ask — date, action, or decision}.

{Sign-off}
```

## Style rules

- **Echo their language.** If they said "ramp time" twice on the call, write "ramp time" — not "onboarding speed."
- **One ask, not three.** Three asks = zero asks.
- **Make the ask specific and easy to say yes to.** "Can we get 30 min next Tuesday or Wednesday?" beats "Let me know when you want to chat."
- **Length: under 150 words.** Cut anything that doesn't move the deal.
- **No "circling back" or "touching base."** Cut on sight.

## When to stop

You're done when the user could send the draft as-is and a busy buyer would (a) read it in 30 seconds, (b) feel understood, and (c) know exactly what's being asked. If any of those three is missing, revise.
